- **Networking Types, Topologies, and Appliances**
    - What is a network?→Two or more connected computer systems.
    - 
    - What are nodes in a network?→Devices that send, receive, or forward data.
    - 
    - What are links in a network?→Communication pathways between nodes.
    - 
    - What is an end system?→A node that sends or receives data.
    - 
    - What is an intermediate node?→A node that forwards data (e.g., router, switch).
    - 
    - What is a client-server network?→Network where servers provide resources, clients consume them.
    - 
    - What is a peer-to-peer network?→Network where each device acts as both client and server.
    - 
    - What is a network appliance?→A device with software for a specific network role.
    - 
    - Give examples of network appliances.→Switch, router, firewall, load balancer, proxy.
    - 
    - What is a network application?→Software that provides a network service (e.g., file sharing, email).
    - 
    - What is a network function?→Special property or configuration (e.g., VPN, QoS).
    - 
    - What is a LAN?→Local Area Network, confined to one site.
    - 
    - What is a WAN?→Wide Area Network, connects multiple networks over large areas.
    - 
    - What is a SOHO network?→Small Office/Home Office network, small-scale LAN.
    - 
    - What is an enterprise LAN?→Large LAN with hundreds or thousands of devices.
    - 
    - What is a datacenter network?→Network mainly hosting servers and storage.
    - 
    - What is network topology?→The arrangement of nodes and links in a network.
    - 
    - What is a physical topology?→Actual layout of network cables/devices.
    - 
    - What is a logical topology?→How data flows through the network.
    - 
    - Describe a point-to-point topology.→Direct link between two nodes.
    - 
    - What is a star topology?→All nodes connect to a central device.
    - 
    - What is a mesh topology?→Devices are interconnected, with multiple paths.
    - 
    - What is a full mesh topology?→Every node connects to every other node.
    - 
    - What is a partial mesh topology?→Only some nodes have direct connections to others.
    - 
    - What is a hub-and-spoke topology?→Central device connects to multiple remote sites.
- **OSI Model Concepts**
    - What does OSI stand for?→Open Systems Interconnection.
    - 
    - How many layers are in the OSI model?→Seven.
    - 
    - What is the purpose of the OSI model?→To standardize networking functions.
    - 
    - Name the seven OSI layers (bottom to top).→Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application.
    - 
    - What is encapsulation?→Wrapping data with protocol information at each layer.
    - 
    - What is decapsulation?→Removing protocol headers/trailers to extract the original data.
    - 
    - What is a PDU?→Protocol Data Unit, the data at each OSI layer.
    - 
    - What is the PDU at the Physical layer?→Bits.
    - 
    - What is the PDU at the Data Link layer?→Frame.
    - 
    - What is the PDU at the Network layer?→Packet or Datagram.
    - 
    - What is the PDU at the Transport layer?→Segment.
    - 
    - What does the Physical layer do?→Transmits raw bits over media.
    - 
    - Name a device at the Physical layer. >>>
        - Hub, 
        - repeater, 
        - media converter.
    - 
    - What does the Data Link layer do?→Provides node-to-node data transfer and error detection.
    - 
    - What is a MAC address?→Unique hardware address for network devices.
    - 
    - Name a device at the Data Link layer.→Switch, bridge, NIC, wireless AP.
    - 
    - What does the Network layer do?→Routes packets between networks.
    - 
    - Name a device at the Network layer.→Router.
    - 
    - What does the Transport layer do?→Ensures reliable data delivery and multiplexing.
    - 
    - What is a port number?→Number identifying a network application/service.
    - 
    - What does the Session layer do?→Manages sessions (dialogs) between applications.
    - 
    - What does the Presentation layer do?→Translates, encrypts, and compresses data.
    - 
    - What does the Application layer do?→Provides network services to user applications.
    - 
    - Give an example of an Application layer protocol.→HTTP, FTP, SMTP.
    - 
    - What layer handles reliable delivery?→Transport layer.

- **Troubleshooting Methodology**
    - Why use a structured troubleshooting methodology?→Ensures problems are resolved efficiently and consistently.
    - 
    - What is the first step in CompTIA’s troubleshooting process?→Identify the problem.
    - 
    - What is the second step?→Establish a theory of probable cause.
    - 
    - What is the third step?→Test the theory to determine cause.
    - 
    - What is the fourth step?→Establish a plan of action.
    - 
    - What is the fifth step?→Implement the solution or escalate.
    - 
    - What is the sixth step?→Verify full system functionality and implement preventive measures.
    - 
    - What is the seventh step?→Document findings, actions, and outcomes.
    - 
    - What should you do when gathering info?→Ask users, check documentation, examine logs.
    - 
    - What are open questions?→Questions allowing users to explain in their own words.
    - 
    - What are closed questions?→Questions with yes/no or specific answers.
    - 
    - What does “duplicate the problem” mean?→Reproduce the issue on a test or the user’s system.
    - 
    - Why check if anything has changed?→Changes often cause new problems.
    - 
    - What is “scope” in troubleshooting?→The area or number of systems affected.
    - 
    - What does “question the obvious” mean?→Check for simple, common issues first.
    - 
    - What is the top-to-bottom OSI approach?→Testing each OSI layer in order.
    - 
    - What is the divide and conquer approach?→Start with the most likely layer, work up or down as needed.
    - 
    - What is escalation?→Referring the problem to someone else if needed.
    - 
    - What is a workaround?→Temporary fix if full solution isn’t possible.
    - 
    - Why document troubleshooting steps?→Helps others, avoids repeating mistakes, tracks solutions.
    - 
    - When should you escalate a problem?→When it’s beyond your knowledge or responsibility.
    - 
    - What are preventive measures?→Steps to avoid the problem happening again.
    - 
    - What is a “known good” device?→Device or cable you know works, used for testing.
    - 
    - What is “test by substitution”?→Replace a suspected faulty part with a known good one.
    - 
    - Why verify full functionality after a fix?→To ensure the issue is resolved and nothing else is broken.
